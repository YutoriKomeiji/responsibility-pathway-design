# RPD Falsification and Theory Revision Protocol v0.1

## Status

This document defines provisional conditions under which RPD propositions, patterns, composition rules, and evaluation claims should be challenged, narrowed, revised, suspended, or retired.

RPD is a developing design framework. A framework that can only reinterpret every result as support is not adequately testable.

## 1. Purpose

The protocol prevents four forms of theory protection:

1. treating every failure as incorrect implementation;
2. treating every success as confirmation of the whole framework;
3. changing definitions after evidence is observed without recording the change;
4. retaining patterns or claims after their useful scope has materially collapsed.

## 2. Objects open to challenge

The following objects may be challenged independently:

- responsibility-pathway concepts;
- finding classes in the transformation kernel;
- objective and requirement derivation rules;
- individual design patterns;
- anti-pattern labels;
- composition relations and invariants;
- evaluation dimensions and metrics;
- assurance levels and claim structures;
- monitoring and reopening rules;
- empirical propositions;
- claims about RPD's distinct contribution.

Evidence against one object does not automatically invalidate every other object. Conversely, support for one object does not validate the entire framework.

## 3. Disconfirmation classes

### F1. Conceptual contradiction

The framework's definitions or rules produce incompatible conclusions under the same declared assumptions.

Response:

- identify the conflicting rules;
- state which scope or assumption produces the contradiction;
- revise, prioritize, or retire the affected rule.

### F2. Non-discriminating diagnosis

RPD labels materially different pathway conditions in the same way and cannot guide distinct design responses.

Response:

- refine the finding classes;
- add discriminating variables;
- narrow the diagnostic claim;
- remove a category that does no useful work.

### F3. Unreliable application

Competent reviewers applying the same protocol reach materially inconsistent findings or recommendations, and the disagreement is not explained by legitimate normative differences or missing evidence.

Response:

- improve operational definitions and training;
- record irreducible judgment;
- limit claims of repeatability;
- suspend the relevant rating or rule if reliability remains inadequate.

### F4. No incremental value

A credible comparator produces equivalent or better pathway understanding and design outcomes with lower burden.

Response:

- identify where RPD duplicates existing methods;
- narrow RPD to the remaining distinctive function;
- integrate rather than compete;
- retire unsupported superiority language.

### F5. Harmful intervention effect

An RPD-informed intervention predictably creates greater delay, concentration of power, surveillance, exclusion, blame transfer, operational fragility, or residual harm than the baseline or alternatives.

Response:

- constrain or suspend the intervention;
- revise the pattern or composition rule;
- add explicit contraindications;
- reassess the underlying objective and affected-party analysis.

### F6. Documentation–practice divergence

A design repeatedly passes artifact review but fails exercise or operation because authority, capability, resources, timing, or access are not real.

Response:

- reject documentation completion as adequate evidence;
- strengthen exercisability requirements;
- revise assurance and monitoring rules;
- return the case to design rather than editing records only.

### F7. Contextual non-transfer

A proposition supported in one domain fails in another because institutional, legal, temporal, cultural, or technical conditions differ.

Response:

- define transfer conditions;
- narrow the proposition's domain;
- create context-specific variants;
- avoid universal claims.

### F8. Burden disproportionality

The time, staffing, cognitive load, friction, or cost introduced by RPD is not proportionate to the stakes or observed benefits.

Response:

- simplify the method;
- define lightweight and full variants;
- limit mandatory artifacts;
- identify contexts where RPD should not be used.

### F9. Affected-party failure

The design appears coherent to owners and reviewers but leaves materially affected parties unable to understand, challenge, obtain remedy, or influence reopening.

Response:

- revise access and contestability requirements;
- include affected-party evidence;
- narrow claims based only on organizational perspectives;
- treat the condition as a pathway failure, not a communication defect alone.

### F10. Residual responsibility loss

The framework improves interruption or rollback but fails to allocate stewardship for irreversible or uncompensated consequences.

Response:

- revise recovery and residual-stewardship patterns;
- prohibit closure based only on technical restoration;
- reopen the design and assurance claim.

## 4. Pattern-level failure conditions

An individual pattern should be revised, suspended, or retired when one or more of the following persist across credible cases:

- its required elements cannot be implemented coherently;
- it produces no distinguishable pathway property;
- its benefits depend on assumptions rarely present in the stated context;
- it creates a recurring anti-pattern elsewhere in the pathway;
- its verification obligations cannot detect practical failure;
- it systematically excludes affected parties;
- a simpler established method provides the same function;
- reviewers cannot distinguish it reliably from another pattern;
- its name implies a stronger effect than evidence supports.

## 5. Invariant failure conditions

A provisional composition invariant should be challenged when:

- a sound design violates the invariant without losing the intended pathway property;
- satisfying the invariant creates greater responsibility discontinuity;
- the invariant cannot be operationalized or reviewed consistently;
- the invariant hides a normative decision as a technical necessity;
- the invariant applies only within an unstated context.

Possible outcomes are:

- retain unchanged;
- refine wording;
- add conditions or exceptions;
- downgrade from invariant to heuristic;
- retire.

## 6. Metric failure conditions

A metric should not be retained merely because it is easy to count.

Challenge a metric when it:

- lacks a clear relationship to the intended pathway property;
- improves while practical responsibility worsens;
- can be gamed through documentation or workflow shaping;
- systematically excludes difficult cases;
- aggregates away important distributional effects;
- encourages unsafe speed or unnecessary delay;
- is too context-dependent for the claimed comparison;
- cannot distinguish implementation from operation.

## 7. Rival explanations

Every empirical study should consider plausible explanations other than RPD, including:

- increased reviewer attention;
- more senior personnel assigned to the intervention;
- extra time and resources;
- general process improvement;
- organizational learning unrelated to RPD;
- participants behaving differently because they are observed;
- changes in technology, policy, staffing, or workload;
- selective reporting.

A result should be attributed to RPD only to the extent that rival explanations are addressed.

## 8. Revision classes

### R0 — Editorial clarification

No substantive rule or claim changes.

### R1 — Operational refinement

Definitions, examples, or procedures become more precise without changing the intended proposition.

### R2 — Scope restriction

A claim or pattern is retained only for a narrower context.

### R3 — Substantive revision

A transformation rule, pattern, invariant, metric, or assurance condition changes materially.

### R4 — Suspension

The object remains documented but should not be used for new claims or designs pending further evidence.

### R5 — Retirement

The object is no longer recommended and remains only for traceability and historical comparison.

## 9. Revision record requirements

Every R2–R5 change should record:

- object identifier and prior version;
- evidence or argument prompting change;
- disconfirmation class;
- affected documents, cases, and claims;
- decision and rationale;
- rejected alternatives;
- migration or reinterpretation guidance;
- responsible human authorizer;
- effective date;
- conditions for reconsideration.

History should not be rewritten to make the framework appear continuously correct.

## 10. Claim downgrade rules

A claim should be downgraded when:

- supporting evidence is withdrawn or found unreliable;
- a stronger comparator eliminates the observed advantage;
- operational evidence contradicts design or exercise evidence;
- the context is narrower than originally reported;
- a material defeater is unresolved;
- a key assumption no longer holds;
- new evidence shows important burden or harm.

Possible downgrades include:

- established in operation → supported only in exercise;
- supported in exercise → supported only by artifact review;
- comparative advantage → no detected difference;
- general proposition → context-bounded proposition;
- recommended pattern → experimental pattern;
- active pattern → suspended pattern.

## 11. Stop conditions for studies and pilots

A study or pilot should have predeclared stop or escalation conditions, including where appropriate:

- unexpected harm or credible threat;
- loss of lawful or ethical basis;
- inability to exercise required fallback authority;
- evidence integrity failure;
- participant withdrawal or retaliation risk;
- unacceptable operational disruption;
- material deviation from the approved scope;
- unresolved conflict of interest;
- inability to protect sensitive operational information.

Stopping a study is not evidence that the framework succeeded or failed. The reason and consequences must be analyzed separately.

## 12. Publication and repository discipline

Public reports should include:

- null and negative findings;
- scope restrictions;
- unresolved disagreements;
- changes made after data collection began;
- retired or suspended objects;
- comparator strengths;
- evidence unavailable for public release;
- claims not supported by the study.

Repository navigation should distinguish active, experimental, suspended, and retired material when those statuses begin to differ.

## 13. Theory-revision cycle

```text
observation or challenge
  → classify the potential disconfirmation
  → inspect evidence and rival explanations
  → identify affected theory object
  → retain, refine, restrict, revise, suspend, or retire
  → update dependent artifacts and claims
  → record human authorization
  → test the revised object
```

## 14. Boundary

This protocol does not prove that RPD is falsifiable in every philosophical sense. It establishes practical conditions under which evidence must be allowed to reduce, narrow, or remove RPD claims rather than being absorbed as unconditional support.
