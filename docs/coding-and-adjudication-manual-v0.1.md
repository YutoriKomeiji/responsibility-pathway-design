# RPD Coding and Adjudication Manual v0.1

## Purpose

This manual defines a provisional procedure for coding responsibility-pathway cases consistently enough to support comparison, disagreement analysis, and theory revision.

Coding is an interpretive research activity. Agreement does not prove validity, and disagreement does not automatically indicate coder failure. Some disagreements reveal ambiguous concepts, insufficient evidence, or context that the codebook does not yet represent.

## Coding object

Code bounded transitions and pathway properties rather than whole organizations or systems.

A coding unit should state:

- case identifier and version;
- transition identifier;
- source span or observation event;
- actor or institution;
- pathway property;
- code assigned;
- evidence tier;
- confidence;
- alternative code considered;
- rationale and limitations.

## Core code families

### P1 — Authority–capability alignment

- P1.0: no relevant authority identified;
- P1.1: authority named but capability absent or inaccessible;
- P1.2: capability exists but authority is ambiguous or delayed;
- P1.3: authority and capability align under declared conditions;
- P1.U: unknown or insufficient evidence.

### P2 — Intervention timing

- P2.0: no usable intervention point;
- P2.1: intervention exists after material option expiry;
- P2.2: intervention timing is conditional or unreliable;
- P2.3: intervention is exercisable within the declared decision window;
- P2.U: unknown.

### P3 — Evidence continuity

- P3.0: relevant decisions or transitions cannot be reconstructed;
- P3.1: partial evidence exists but material gaps remain;
- P3.2: evidence is reconstructable with limitations;
- P3.3: evidence continuity supports the declared review need;
- P3.U: unknown.

### P4 — Returnability

- P4.0: no accountable return point;
- P4.1: nominal return point without usable intake or authority;
- P4.2: conditional return route with material limitations;
- P4.3: accountable human or institutional return point is exercisable;
- P4.U: unknown.

### P5 — Contestability

- P5.0: affected parties cannot understand or challenge the transition;
- P5.1: challenge route exists only formally or is inaccessible;
- P5.2: challenge is possible with notable burden or scope limits;
- P5.3: challenge is accessible, reviewable, and connected to remedy;
- P5.U: unknown.

### P6 — Recovery capacity

- P6.0: no correction, restoration, compensation, or reform route;
- P6.1: technical rollback exists without responsibility recovery;
- P6.2: partial recovery exists with unresolved ownership or resources;
- P6.3: recovery actions and ownership are defined and exercisable;
- P6.U: unknown.

### P7 — Residual stewardship

- P7.0: irreducible or remaining impact has no owner;
- P7.1: residual impact is acknowledged but ownership is nominal;
- P7.2: stewardship exists with scope or duration limits;
- P7.3: residual impact has an accountable, resourced steward;
- P7.U: unknown.

### P8 — Assumption validity

- P8.0: material assumptions are absent or contradicted;
- P8.1: assumptions are implicit or stale;
- P8.2: assumptions are recorded but incompletely monitored;
- P8.3: assumptions, expiry conditions, and review triggers are explicit;
- P8.U: unknown.

### P9 — Cross-organizational handoff integrity

- P9.0: responsibility disappears or becomes untraceable at handoff;
- P9.1: handoff is documented without acceptance or intervention clarity;
- P9.2: handoff is partially specified with unresolved dependencies;
- P9.3: authority, evidence, timing, acceptance, and recovery obligations transfer explicitly;
- P9.U: unknown.

### P10 — Burden distribution

- P10.0: burden is unobserved or excluded from the case model;
- P10.1: burden is shifted to actors without authority or resources;
- P10.2: burden is recognized but uneven or weakly mitigated;
- P10.3: burden distribution is examined, justified, and monitored;
- P10.U: unknown.

## Anti-pattern coding

Anti-patterns should be coded as:

- present;
- absent with supporting evidence;
- plausible but unresolved;
- not applicable;
- not observable from available evidence.

Do not code an anti-pattern as absent merely because the case record does not mention it.

## Pattern coding

For each pattern, distinguish:

- named in design only;
- implemented;
- exercised;
- observed in operation;
- challenged or defeated;
- modified;
- withdrawn.

A pattern name in a policy or architecture document is not evidence of exercisability.

## Confidence scale

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| C0 | Speculative; evidence does not support stable coding |
| C1 | Weak; one limited source or substantial inference |
| C2 | Moderate; multiple sources or direct but bounded observation |
| C3 | Strong within declared scope; converging evidence and limited unresolved conflict |

Confidence concerns the coding decision, not the desirability or quality of the pathway.

## Missing and conflicting evidence

Use explicit states:

- missing;
- inaccessible;
- withheld;
- contradictory;
- superseded;
- not collected;
- not applicable.

Do not replace missing evidence with the midpoint of a scale. Preserve asymmetry: absence of evidence may itself be operationally meaningful, but it is not always evidence of absence.

## Independent coding procedure

1. Freeze the case packet version.
2. Train coders on at least two calibration cases.
3. Code independently without viewing the other coder's decisions.
4. Record source anchors and rationales.
5. Compare codes only after independent completion.
6. Calculate agreement for eligible code families.
7. Adjudicate disagreements without deleting the original decisions.
8. Record whether the disagreement arose from evidence, interpretation, codebook ambiguity, scope, or error.
9. Update the case, codebook, or theory only through a versioned change.

## Agreement analysis

Raw percent agreement may be reported but should not be the only statistic. Depending on the data and sample size, studies may use Cohen's kappa, weighted kappa, Krippendorff's alpha, or another justified measure.

Before interpreting a coefficient, report:

- number of cases and coding units;
- prevalence of each code;
- missing and not-applicable rates;
- whether codes are nominal or ordinal;
- confidence intervals where feasible;
- coder training and independence;
- adjudication rate.

A low coefficient may reflect prevalence imbalance or conceptual ambiguity. A high coefficient may reflect an oversimplified codebook.

## Adjudication outcomes

Each disagreement should end in one of these states:

- A1: one code retained after source correction;
- A2: one code retained after interpretive adjudication;
- A3: both codes retained as a legitimate ambiguity;
- A4: coding unit split into narrower units;
- A5: code changed to unknown or insufficient evidence;
- A6: codebook requires revision;
- A7: theory proposition or pattern boundary requires revision.

## Adjudication record

Record:

- original coder decisions;
- source anchors;
- disagreement class;
- adjudicator and independence limits;
- discussion summary;
- final disposition;
- case or codebook version change;
- unresolved minority interpretation;
- effect on analysis.

Consensus should not erase a defensible minority interpretation.

## Calibration and drift

Long-running studies should periodically recode a stable calibration set. Trigger review when:

- agreement drops materially;
- code usage shifts without case-mix explanation;
- new sectors create repeated ambiguity;
- unknown codes become unusually common;
- one coder systematically assigns higher or lower states;
- adjudication repeatedly changes the same definition.

## Research boundaries

Coding results should not be presented as legal liability determinations, safety ratings, fairness certifications, organizational rankings, or proof of causal effect. Final interpretation, publication, and any external consequence remain subject to human review and applicable research governance.
