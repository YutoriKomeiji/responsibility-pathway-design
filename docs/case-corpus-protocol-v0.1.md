# RPD Case Corpus Protocol v0.1

## Purpose

This protocol defines how to assemble a comparative corpus for Responsibility Pathway Design (RPD) research without converting a convenience sample into evidence of general validity.

The corpus is intended to support structured comparison, pattern testing, negative-case analysis, theory revision, and later replication. It is not a certification dataset, benchmark of organizational quality, or proof that RPD improves safety, legality, fairness, effectiveness, or social acceptance.

## Corpus unit

The primary unit is a **bounded responsibility-pathway case**, not an organization, model, product, or incident in the abstract.

Each case should identify:

- the sociotechnical process under study;
- the relevant judgment and execution transitions;
- the actors and institutions involved;
- the affected parties;
- the temporal and organizational boundaries;
- the evidence window;
- the pathway property or proposition being examined;
- the reason the case is included.

A single organization may contribute multiple cases when the pathways, transitions, or evidence windows differ materially.

## Case families

A balanced corpus should include more than successful or well-documented interventions.

### C1 — Breakdown cases

Cases in which authority, capability, evidence, intervention, recovery, or residual stewardship became disconnected.

### C2 — Repair cases

Cases in which a pathway weakness was identified and a repair was attempted.

### C3 — Stable-operation cases

Cases with repeated evidence that a responsibility-pathway property remained exercisable over time.

### C4 — Null cases

Cases in which an RPD-informed design produced no material difference relative to a credible alternative.

### C5 — Negative cases

Cases in which an RPD-informed intervention introduced new burdens, delays, exclusion, authority confusion, or other adverse effects.

### C6 — Rival-success cases

Cases in which a non-RPD method produced equal or better observed results.

### C7 — Boundary cases

Cases that test whether RPD concepts remain useful across different sectors, jurisdictions, organizational structures, or stakes.

### C8 — Inconclusive cases

Cases where evidence is insufficient, contradictory, inaccessible, or too confounded to support a directional conclusion.

## Inclusion criteria

A case may enter the corpus only when:

1. the pathway and transition scope can be bounded;
2. at least one responsibility-pathway proposition can be stated;
3. the evidence basis and its limitations can be described;
4. the case is not included solely because it supports RPD;
5. affected-party interests and access constraints can be considered;
6. the source and permission conditions are known;
7. the case can be represented without exposing prohibited or unnecessary personal information;
8. uncertainty and missing evidence can be retained rather than filled by inference.

## Exclusion criteria

Exclude or quarantine cases when:

- the pathway cannot be distinguished from general organizational performance;
- the only evidence is promotional or self-certifying;
- the case depends on unverifiable allegations;
- confidentiality cannot be preserved;
- legal or contractual restrictions prohibit the intended analysis;
- affected-party risk from inclusion exceeds the research value;
- the case would require fabricating missing transitions, actors, or outcomes;
- the same event is duplicated under multiple labels without declared linkage.

Exclusion should be logged. Excluded cases may reveal selection bias and should not disappear silently.

## Sampling strategy

The corpus should declare which sampling logic is used.

| Strategy | Purpose | Main risk |
|---|---|---|
| Criterion sampling | Ensure minimum evidence and pathway clarity | Over-selects documented cases |
| Maximum-variation sampling | Test concepts across diverse contexts | Differences may overwhelm comparison |
| Matched-case sampling | Compare similar pathways with different designs | Matching may conceal important context |
| Extreme-case sampling | Stress-test theory at high stakes or unusual conditions | Weak generalizability |
| Negative-case sampling | Seek disconfirming evidence | Availability and publication bias |
| Longitudinal sampling | Observe pathway degradation and repair | Attrition and changing context |
| Replication sampling | Test prior coding and findings independently | Access and coordination cost |

Convenience sampling may be used for pilot work only when labeled explicitly.

## Minimum corpus balance

A corpus release should report:

- counts by case family;
- counts by sector and pathway type;
- public, confidential, synthetic, and mixed-source cases;
- positive, null, negative, rival-success, and inconclusive findings;
- cases excluded and reasons for exclusion;
- cases with affected-party evidence;
- cases with independent or external evidence;
- cases coded by more than one reviewer.

No fixed numerical quota is prescribed in v0.1. A corpus dominated by one family or source type should not support broad claims.

## Evidence tiers

Each case should record its strongest available evidence tier.

| Tier | Evidence condition |
|---|---|
| D0 | Synthetic or illustrative material only |
| D1 | Public documentary evidence with unresolved gaps |
| D2 | Multiple documentary sources or structured interviews |
| D3 | Exercise, simulation, or direct process observation |
| D4 | Bounded operational evidence over time |
| D5 | Independent replication or multi-site longitudinal evidence |

A higher tier does not imply that all propositions in the case are supported equally.

## Source provenance

Every source should record:

- source identifier;
- source type;
- creator or custodian;
- collection date;
- version or capture date;
- access and permission status;
- transformations applied;
- redactions;
- reliability limits;
- conflicts of interest;
- retention and withdrawal conditions.

Derived summaries should never replace the source-provenance record.

## Confidentiality and minimization

Corpus design should apply data minimization before anonymization.

Collect only information needed to examine the declared pathway proposition. Remove or generalize names, exact dates, locations, rare job titles, internal identifiers, and other indirect identifiers unless analytically necessary and permitted.

Use a separate controlled linkage record when re-identification is legitimately required. Public corpus materials should not contain the linkage key.

Synthetic cases must be labeled as synthetic. Composite cases must state that multiple sources were combined and must not imply that the composite describes a real identifiable event.

## Case versioning

Each case should have:

- a stable case identifier;
- semantic or date-based version;
- status: proposed, admitted, coded, reviewed, released, corrected, withdrawn, or archived;
- change log;
- superseded versions retained where lawful and appropriate;
- reason for correction or withdrawal.

Corrections should distinguish source correction, coding correction, interpretation revision, and theory revision.

## Cross-case comparison rules

Cross-case analysis should not compare raw scores without checking:

- pathway scope equivalence;
- stakes and option-expiry differences;
- organizational and jurisdictional context;
- evidence-tier differences;
- affected-party access;
- intervention maturity;
- observation duration;
- missing-data patterns;
- whether the same code was interpreted consistently.

Where cases are not commensurable, the analysis should remain configurational or qualitative rather than forcing a common ranking.

## Negative and null evidence

Negative, null, rival-success, and inconclusive cases must remain visible in corpus summaries.

A case should not be reclassified as successful merely because documentation improved. A pathway outcome may remain unchanged, worsen, or shift burden to another actor.

## Governance

Corpus roles should distinguish, where feasible:

- source custodian;
- case preparer;
- coder;
- adjudicator;
- affected-party reviewer or representative;
- release decision owner;
- withdrawal contact.

The same person may hold multiple roles in a small study, but the lack of independence must be disclosed.

## Release gates

Before any corpus release, verify:

1. permission and confidentiality conditions;
2. de-identification and indirect-identification risk;
3. source provenance and transformation history;
4. coding completeness and unresolved disagreements;
5. inclusion of negative and null cases;
6. claim boundaries;
7. correction and withdrawal route;
8. human authorization for release.

## Research status

This protocol is provisional. It requires testing through pilot corpus construction, independent coding, affected-party review, and external replication. It does not authorize collection, disclosure, publication, or operational use of confidential case material.
